Erik Lund

Most air combats (80% is the number that sticks to mind) are ambushes. When the energetically advantaged plane detects first, it converts energy into positional advantage. The position behind the detected target is both the easiest to reach and the easiest shot –and this applies to missiles just as much as fighters.
When a disadvantaged aircraft detects first, that disadvantage remains dangerous. The best defence is to counter the opponents’ energetic advantage by whatever means of maximising velocity/altitude change are available.
The higher the engine power, the lower the drag, the higher the rate of climb, the better the fighter. There is very little room here for a back seater. Now, an interceptor assumed to have a privileged position over any possible target is a very different matter, which is why carrier fighters tend to be two-seaters, and why I am now prepared to argue about Fairey fighters and why Correlli Barnett is a stupid-head.

ajay

Why no rear guns on fighters?
Used to be: because of unacceptable weight penalties. Ditch the gun, the turret and the gunner, and you’ll have enough speed that you’ll be able to get behind the other guy instead. (Boulton Paul Defiant. Not A Good Fighter.)
Now: who uses guns? Combat takes place at missile ranges – ten, twenty, thirty miles or more. If there’s an enemy right behind you at gun range, you don’t want to hang around lining up a shot from your rear gun, because you have about half a second left to live, so you want to break hard.

What about rear-firing missiles?
The first thing a missile fired directly to the rear is going to hit is the exhaust and turbulence immediately behind you. This isn’t good for them.

What about ones that loop around?
Air to air missiles come in two classes. Short-range ones are IR homing; they pick up the heat of an exhaust. Long range ones are radar homing, using either the fighter’s radar, their own radar or both.
Now, IR homers are obviously fire-and-forget; but they have to pick up the target first. You have to point them at it. And they’re carried pointing forwards, because that’s the way they’re launched. So you need to point your aircraft at the target, tell the missile “there it is, go get him” and then launch.
Radar homers – you need to “lock” your radar onto the target, and your radar’s in the nose of your aircraft. So the same applies.

Erik Lund

Looking at the world war…
i) “The Defiant had no forward guns” is a red herring, probably out of Hawker-Siddeley, whose rival type could take a wing gun. Boulton Paul answered that by pointing out that 1 gun was hardly sufficient under modern conditions, hence the 4-gun turret, and the “Wolverhampton Dreadnought,” which would have had a 4xHispano 20mm turret(400lbs of rotary weight for the guns alone!)
Since the Defiant’s turret was 360, with a neat selective interruptor, the Boulton Paul plane had forward armament when needed. (Caveat: the splatbook I’m citing only _specifies_ this for the Defiant II, so I might be wrong about the type that fought the Battle of Britain, though I cannot imagine why I would be.)
It has been suggested that most Defiant losses in the Battle may have been a result of blowing overstressed main fuses/being out of ammo than through tactical inadequacies.
ii) The Defiant has been usefully described as an attempt to expand the intercept envelope by improving on the weapon system rather than ‘plane performance. It was intended for killing bombers/recon types rather than fighters, and has this in common with night fighters and many carrier fighters.
iii) That said, I would be interested in tracking down the evolution of the design and seeing if the Bristol Fighter was an influence. Certainly the Defiant was undergoing proving as an army cooperation type when the war broke out. But the key issue here would be radio and the perceived need for a dedicated operator, a rapidly changing situation.
iv) Remote control rear-facing weapons brought old-timey engineers right to the bleeding edge of contemporary automated control technology. The Me210, Vickers Windsor and perhaps the Lincoln had experimental installations, but there was nothing earlier that I can think of. This is a much understudied subject, considering how important these “computer” things have turned out to be, although there was a good book out of MIT a few years back. I’m ashamed to admit that I’m drawing a blank on the author’s name, though. Need more coffee.
v) The many twin-engined fighters of the war raise separate issues. In general the designers were trying to pack in so much engine power that they could have their cake and eat it, too. This is how the P-61 Black Widow ended up with a wing span comparable to a 1939 medium bomber, and a moment of inertia to match.

yorksranter

Errr….this precisely recapitulates the USAF’s fighter doctrine of the Vietnam era, which held that a) missiles are everything, b) beyond-visual-range radar-guided missiles are better, and c) our superior technology will prevail. Hence the main point was to be the first to the battle, to identify the enemy on radar, and launch medium-range (Sparrow) missiles, then stand well back while the enemy tried to evade them and you tried to evade the enemy’s.

Unfortunately the North Vietnamese air force in their MiGs, and their Soviet advisors, took a different view; they held that a) the Americans have really good rockets and so b) we better do something else, which was to endeavour to avoid step 2 above, or failing that, to launch their medium-range missiles first in order to start your fighters evading (and pointing their eyes and radars in the other direction) and then to bore in on your fighters at maximum speed and “merge”, as they say in the trade, which means to convert the battle into a dogfight.

This worked embarrassingly well, with the result that the USAF and USN did a major rethink of their tactics, technology, training, and aircraft, which concluded that you better be good at the difficult stuff, however great your widgets are.

Some would say that over-reliance on Our Superior Technology is a lasting American trait.
