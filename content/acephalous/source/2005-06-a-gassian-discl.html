A Gassian Disclaimer, or An Apology for Yesterday's, um, OED-Inspired Madness

Last week I asked you to name whose prose enlivens yours.  Now I'd like to know whose deadens it.  As yesterday's Gassian fiasco suggests, no writer has a more deleterious effect on my prose than Gass.  My brain wraps itself around his prose and is unable to unwend itself for days, nay, weeks afterward.  It's fairly funny to me how horrible I write after reading Gass, especially since I so enjoy reading him.  Who doesn't love prose of powerfully alliterative and argumentative density?  I know I don't not.

[That said, writing with OED-inspired momentum, as I did yesterday, is a thrilling exercise, one I recommend to everyone.  I don't, however, recommend publishing the results of said exercises publically unless you're willing to 1) plug your ears to the torrents of abuse from readers who demand some sense in their nonsense, 2) be thought an addict of some new ADHD medication, 3) supply others with scripts for your new ADHD medication on pain of pain (which is pain enough for me), or 4) lose your regular readers to less violently idiotic sites like FrontPage Magazine.]

Hagoort demonstrated that "people know the grammatical data about a noun approximately 40 milliseconds earlier than they [utter] the first syllable of a word." (With 40 milliseconds being such a frickin' long time, it's no coincidence that Hagoort works at the Max Planck Institute for Psycholinguistics.) According to Hagoort's 1998 article in Science, This knowledge includes information about the meaning of words, their syntactic properties (such as word class), and their phonological properties (such as their phonemes and syllable structure). All this information is stored in a component of long-term memory that is usually referred to as the mental lexicon. During speaking, the mental lexicon is accessed automatically at very high speed to select words that express the intended meaning, and to retrieve their syntactic and phonological properties....A central unresolved question concerns the orchestration in real time of the retrieval of the distinct types of linguistic knowledge required to produce fluent speech.
