Why Buffy Is Funny, Family Guy Isn't, and Warren Ellis Is Capable of So Much More

Parody requires more than a fine idea.  Take my recent "Fuck Act Theory" posts.  In addition to being funny on its face, the parody works because of its consonance with actual "Speech Act Theory."  The focus on marriage as a spoken contract jibes with centuries of thought about the necessity of consummating a marriage.  Now do you see why it's funny?  What aren't you laughing already. 

Some people.  Anyhow, I write this not to toot my horn.  For shame!  Thinking this further evidence of my penchant for self-promotion.    I'm writing because this afternoon I finished a disappointing Star Trek parody.  Here's the idea:

Ray Winstone is an excellent British actor who tends to play working-class London psychotics.  Even when he's the good guy, he's usually fucking terrifying.  Only once in (my) recent memory has he played against type in film, as the second safecracker in Sexy Beast.  See, everyone first saw Ray Winstone as a teenager whipping other kids to death with a pool ball in a sock, screaming WHO'S THE DADDY NOW!!  WHO'S THE FUCKING DADDY NOW!  I'M THE DADDY NOW!  He's a force of nature, a thing driven by beer and cigarettes and kicking fucking heads in.  He's got a chubby face and little boy's eyes that, in the moment, go black and dead like shark's eyes.  Reduces London English to a series of grunts and yelps. He is, in fact, the Greatest Living Londoner.

And the whole thing unfurled as a I spoke, like a flag that someone has wiped on their arse first.  Insisting on smoking on the bridge.  Ray Winstone has to have his fags (which in Britain means "cigarettes"—it's the first term we purge from our vocabulary when we travel to America).  And beer.  "Have one of our futuristic blue drinks with no alcohol, sir?"  "Fuck off.  Fucking pint, son.  Right fucking now."  That standard Star Trek moment when somone asks the captain if they can go now, and spouts some techno-bollocks at him about how the course is set and all that.  "Well fucking go on then, son.  Twat."

So says the vibrating Warren Ellis—now obliged to never change his banner lest I look the fool—in the introductory material to Switchblade Honey.  The book's damn funny but ain't much of a book.  Barely a parody even.*  Why bad mouth one of my favorite writers?  Because I've picked up the habit of beating bloody anyone who fails to live up to the expectations they've created.  (Have you read nothing I've written about left homophobia and misogyny?  Preoccupied pisser.)  I'm increasingly convinced that my dissatisfaction—nay, my inability to find funny the decontextualized "humor" of Family Guy and The Simpsons comes from the inexhaustive parroting of the object of their derision.  What do I mean?

All the jokes are single shooters.  Funny or not they don't move the plot forward.  They're minimally contextualized absurdisms whose import disappears the moment they're off-screen.  Which is fine.  Dandy even.  But it guarantees the show will be no more than fleetingly funny.  As satisfying as a bowl of sugar drenched in milk.  The high produced bottoms out with such depressing regularity that I'd rather not tender the initial investment.  (See what I mean about my ol' aversion to the law of diminishing returns?  I want to see it burn.)  Family Guy produces a high that lasts no longer than the injection.  From ha-ha to keening in forty-seconds flat. 

I oppose such crap to the expansive humor of something genius like Office Space.  The first time through you don't get the hype.  The second time—on HBO during lunch one day—you chuckle a few times.  By the third—repeated 3 a.m. viewings on HBOW-C9801—you can barely hold your breath or the cough medicine down.  By the third time through Family Guy or The Simpsons you're repeating the best lines as a part of a hipster pair- or social-bonding ritual.  It's ceased to amuse but let that on and you'll blow it in front of everybody. 

Such parody fails because it mocks without creating viewer and/or reader investment in the object of mockery.  Buffy remains funny because the humor works in a particular context.  You can't quote it out of context to impress Soy Mocha Latte Decaf with the adorable bob.  I could make a grand statement about Family Guy signalling the end of connectedness—of it being the evidence of capitalism's success in alienating us from context altogether. 

But this is a post is about Ellis' Switchblade Honey.  It would've been better had Ellis attempted to pull some recognizable Star Trek stunt.  He opens with a viable scenario but it quickly spirals into the absurd.  You can tell the jokes came before the narrative because the narrative's tacked-on.  Someone smart could scold me with a quick "But that's the point.  Star Trek proves that universe contains infinite machina ready to deus ex the unitarded from situations hopeless and ironic!"  (Of all the silly posts I've written this is by far the densest.  Larded with underdeveloped ideas it is.) 

Since you've read this far I offer my thanks in form of 1) this white and milk chocolate chip "racial harmony" cookie and 2) this bit of dialogue from Switchblade Honey.  Ray Winstone-Pickard and his crew are about to engage the inhuman "Chasta" in battle.  The pilot informs Cap' that the Chasta "are blanketing communications—looks like their usual weird psychological stuff."  The first signal arrives:

WE ARE FUCKING YOUR WIVES

The battle continues.  As the engineer informs Cap' that "thermal damage is going to be an issue" the second arrives:

WHILE YOU ARE OUT HERE FIGHTING IN VAIN YOUR CHILDREN ARE BEING ABUSED BY FAMOUS EARTH ACTORS LIKE BART SIMPSON.

As the crew turns tail to run the third signal arrives:

YOUR WIFE IS ON MICKEY MOUSE'S PHALLUS.  SHE LIKES IT BETTER.

Funny stuff.  I only wish it could stand up to repeated readings. 

*Adam knows more about parody than the lot of us.  And because Ellis is a countryman, 'haps he can tell us whether Switchblade Honey works better in Britain.  I doubt it does, since the failure's belongs not to the wit but the plot.)

For that matter, the plot of Office Space is clever, but not really a world-beater. Does Michael Bolton resenting the performer Michael Bolton play into the context of the plot, or is it just a great gag? Surprise is a huge part of comedy, and dropping in a joke out of nowhere is a great--but very difficult--way of achieving that.

I think you're dead wrong about humour more generally, though, Scott. The Simpsons does go off the boil in later series; and Family Guy is too disconnected and underwritten to really work. There's also a real issue at the heart of Python's humour: British retreat from Empire, I'd say; the anxiety of social uncertainty, the erosion of the class system, people not knowing where they are, quite how to act, what's going on. This is what the Dada-ist oddity of Python captures so very well: figures of authority acting in bizarre ways, discourses of authority being subverted.

But there's a real social phenomenon in the parrotting of favourite lines of comedy. That's certainly how Python worked when I was at school in the 70s: we memorised great screeds of it, and it acted a sort of social glue, it provided an idiom in which we could all cohere. This isn't, in other words, just a matter of people without imagination dully mouthing these words; it's an active, often creative intertextual appropriation. Or it can be.
