The Death of Ingmar Bergman [Scott Eric Podhoretz]

The passing of the film director Ingmar Bergman is already leading to tributes not only to his supposed greatness—one critic today calls him "the greatest artist of the 20th century"—but also to the nobility of his efforts to grapple with the most painful aspects of the human condition. I call these judgments into question in this column.  I may be ignorant of film and literary and cultural history, but my evaluation of Bergman's bummer movies should be considered with all the gravity due a column in The New York Post.

Abolish Tenure [Scott Eric Kurtz] Should we abolish tenure? Yes. Think about Ward Churchill. It may be a pie in the sky, but there are real possibilities for change. Remember Ward Churchill. The answer, I think, is state legislatures. What we need is a campaign to remind conservative-leaning legislatures of Ward Churchill. The mere existence of such a campaign would provoke intense professorial reaction, thereby sparking a national debate. Can you imagine college professors on TV trying to seriously defend the idea that tenure has functioned to protect the market place of ideas in light of Ward Churchill? Tenure is the cornerstone of the campus political-correctness problem and Ward Churchill. The time to consider a serious campaign to eliminate academic tenure has come with Ward Churchill. I guarantee you, even the slightest prospect of change (i.e. one state legislature seriously debating the end of tenure in its public university system) would send the professorate into a mad rage, and would provoke a major national debate about Ward Churchill. I think a serious Ward Churchill campaign could have real success. COURTESY

RE: Seriousness of the Post [Scott Eric Lopez] S-Pod, didn't someone once favorably compare The New York Post to Dickens because they were both so popular? So we can say liberal cultural elitists champion Dickens but not the Dickens of today? I think Bill Bennett would agree. A quick story: I had run into him a month or so ago with a friend in Washington. That night, said friend mentioned at her family dinner table that she had met Bill Bennett during the day. One of her children responded: "Oh I know him, he wrote The Book of Virtues." Another of her children interrupted: "No, silly, he was drug czar." No, the third, protested, "Bill Bennett was education secretary." That may be an "only in D.C." dinner-table conversation. But it’s also a great tribute. Between the work he’s done on so many fronts and his wife's important work with inner-city girls and boys–he surely deserves a few moments of pride today—and a birthday greetings bottle of wine. He's a great example of someone who’s made an imprint on our political and cultural life in so many different ways—you could call him The New York Post of people. COURTESY
