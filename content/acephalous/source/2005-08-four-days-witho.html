Bloggissimus Omnibus, i.e. Every Paragraph a Different Treat (So If You Find One Dull, Skip to the Next)

Four days without regular (by which I mean "constant and dictatorial") access to computing and networking power has made me realize how much of my life depends upon my laptop: dissertating and blogging (an activity which not only encompasses the act of writing on my blog but also the regular reading of others') are the active outlets; reading the news I otherwise only hear from Jon Stewart, keeping tabs on the latest developments in my statistics-heavy hobby and whittling hours upon hours upon hours away on the most trivial matters are the passive ones.  (Though truth be told, for the humanistically inclined any hobby which involves crunching numbers shouldn't be considered "passive."  Still, this one--despite the mathematics involved--is more than a whit indulgent.)  All of that said, all I can say is that I've so much to say that I can hardly decide where to start.

There's the umbicular connection I share with my malfunctioning laptop.  I realize this, recognize the problem and scream inside my head everytime I can't record some mundanity in an ephemeral email to myself (which I'll never read or even remember writing come day's end). 

Then there's the fact that My Hometown (despite my self-identification with Brooklyn and northern New Jersey and the years spent in the Canadian hinterland), what Huey Long compulsively called "The Great State of Louisiana," currently stands more submerged than usual come creole August, and that the estimated death toll rises with every learned estimation.  The emails I've received from friends in the midst of it remain unencouraging.  One friend's father has lost everything, another's mother only materialized after three agonizingly uncommunicative days, and still another's brother remains among the missing.  I could blather on for days about what the tragedy that is My Hometown at this moment, but my blather would only trivialize the magnitude of the events about which I blather.

On a similarly somber subject, Badger (of Badgerings) lost her husband last Monday.  Even though I only know her through that most tenuous of tenuous connections (the polite acknowledgment of the link), I send my sympathies and (were I one to pray) all the prayers I know.  But since I'm not one to pray I'll settle for sending my thoughts, so that she knows her husband's touched lives so distant, so removed, and will not be forgetton.  (And while it may seem untoward to address her grief in so omnibus an entry, since I don't know when I'll have unfettered access again, I think the sentiment outweighs whatever violation of decorum I've committed.  Best to you and yours in your time of trials, Badger.)

Come Monday I decided to attend this year's MLA (so that I've experienced one before I scour another for reasonable job offers) and have no idea how to follow through with this decision.  Do I purchase a ticket, book a hotel room and wander from motel-to-hotel in search of thems that I know and thems that I'd like to?  (Paging Mr. McLemee, Mr. McCann, Mr. Blisset and other English-type-scholars who've experienced such archaic rites.)  Or do I dance like the fly-on-the-wall from one panel to the next and pray for a face I recognize?  Is glomming onto the first friendly face considered impolitic?  (If any readers or fellow Valveteers plan on attending, throw my sanity some life-preservers and spare me some sleepness nights.)

John Bruce continues to skewer others for those things from which he himself suffers so mightily: a overweening sense of self-importance, muddy prose mistakenly believed to be the very model of clarity, and above all presumptuous estimations of the quality of his own "literary" output, complete with imperinent declarations of his own superiority.  (The standard for mocking middle-management, while low in literary circles, remains obscenely high in those of popular culture.  Bruce's "serialized blog novel" showcases his tin ear and absolute incomprehesion with a painfully unironic hammer which, if wielded by anyone else, would make meat of his gristle.  Instead, we're treated to bland aggrandiziments of his petty pseudo-intellectual power-plays which conveniently double as "literary" forays into the culture of middle management.   Mr. Bruce, you self-righteous self-important but utterly ignorant S.O.B., I have for you some words:

Perhaps you ought to ch-ch-check yourself before you wr-wr-wreck yourself.  Or watch Office Space or The Office (preferably Ricky Gervais' BBC version, but its Americanization will suffice).  Even someone as dense as you will realize how unsatisfying your satire's been in light of such shows.  Not that I believe you'll admit as much, being the self-important presidium you ceaselessly declare yourself to be, but at the very least your bestial banality will eat away at you night and day (declarations otherwise notwithstanding), and that's satisfaction enough for me.  (Actually, I know it won't, since you're too dense to see what the rest of the world can't avoid.)

In other news, the Little Womedievalist and I will still be in San Francisco come September 7th.  Tickets for my NoCal performances are still avaible since it appears as if I know no locals but Ray and David.

Ugh.  I have much more to say about Louisiana, The Grudge, recent posts on Cliopatria, the Weblog, and the Philip Roth Festschrift on The Valve.  As soon as my computer rejoins the land of the living spinning and cycling, I'll contribute to the Philip Roth festivities with an unbelievably erudite post whose detenebrative qualities will be remembered for generations. (I mean, really, I've had damn near two weeks to write it.  How could something someone's spent two weeks on be anything less than brilliant?)

On the Situation Outside of New Orleans; or, My First Angry Political Rant I had written a long rant about the deteriorating conditions in my home state of Louisiana. I deleted it. Too much worrying over issues like gun control now that they've become topical. Friends of mine in Baton Rouge want to buy ammunition because they fear that the desperate evacuees from New Orleans may redirect their anger at the massive bureaucratic incompetence responsible for both the failure of the levees and the humanitarian crisis which followed. Redirect it at whom? The nearest available target. The family whose house was battered but not obliviated by the winds and the waves. The person whose credit line afforded them the opportunity to purchase gasoline and escape ahead of the landfall. The woman whose children and grandchildren aren't listed on the rolls of those missing and presumed (for now) unable to phone family and inform them they've weathered the storm. In other words, other victims of the massive bureaucratic incompetence responsible for both the failure of the levees and the humanitarian crisis which followed. Then there are those who will suffer from future bureaucratic incompetence, like those refugees lucky enough to escape one submerged parish or another only to be placed in a staging area in which riots between rival gangs from the Ninth and Seventeenth Wards have broken out. Regularly. For three days now. Perhaps placing people who've already endured unimaginable psychological stress in the same temporary shelters with people who've endured similar horrors but belong to rival gangs is less "bad idea" and more "criminal negligence." It's not as if recent events and current privations would have these refugees working with triggers the adjective "hair" grossly overestimates. This material is representative of what I had written in the other post. Only not nearly so shrill. Or outraged. Or saddened. Or worried. In this entry I've tempered my temper. I'll probably delete this entry come morning, but I needed to vent. And have now. Twice.
