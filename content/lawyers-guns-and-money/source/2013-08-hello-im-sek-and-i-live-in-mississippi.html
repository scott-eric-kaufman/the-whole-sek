      Hello, I’m SEK and I live in Mississippi.

      [ 141 ] August 4, 2013 | SEK

      At least until I find a place in Baton Rouge. I feel obligated to write this post because of the concern espoused in a previous one about my travels in Texas. I attribute making it through unscathed to the gigantic Lincoln Navigator I was piloting: I looked far too wealthy and American to be pulled over.  Now here are some completely random notes:

      • Texas is too big. I’m familiar with its eastern bits — my parents live in Houston and my wife studied at UT — but that stretch between El Paso and San Antonio has no business existing.
      • It doesn’t matter how large your vehicle is when you share it with two howling cats who don’t even have the decency to harmonize. It just doesn’t.
      • The In Our Time podcast is as good as advertised. Given that I can attest to the quality of the material on matters I’m expert in — Joyce, Yeats, Alfred Russel Wallace, the history of evolutionary theory, etc. — I’m going to be quick to flash my vast store of new-found knowledge. (“Did you know there’s no solid evidence Marco Polo existed?” is something I will be saying at a dinner-party in the near future.)
      • Southern California produces better Mexican food than Texas and no cuisine in the world is comparable to Louisiana’s. These are facts that cannot be disputed.

                Can you recommend a good book or article for the layman?

                The question of Jesus as myth is not well received in the circles of biblical scholarship. For two reasons. First, the people who become biblical scholars do so because they are believers. Secondly, after all those years of hard work learning the languages and boning up on all the previous scholarship, to announce that it is all built on a sand is a one-way ticket to loss of status, loss of grants, loss of jobs.

                A.N. Wilson wrote a really interesting book about Paul which I highly recommend to everyone, believer or not. Did you know that Jews constituted perhaps as much as 10% of the population of the Empire? To me, that explains a great deal.

                There is also “Nailed” by David Fitzgerald, but his complete lack of respect for the feelings and traditions of believers will be hugely offensive to many.

                You could also just Google a few things and read some lively current debate. For example: Nazareth was probably uninhabited at the time in question. There were monks – think Dead Sea scroll types – called Nazirites. Nazareth began to grow later. Probably early versions of the Gospels called Jesus a Nazirite, but later generations had no idea what that meant, so the Gospels were amended to make it a place name.

                Other fruitful inquiries: there is no sea in Galilee, but good Greek story-telling involves watery peril, so therefore one was invented. Check out the Wikipedia list of seas and compare the size of Galilee to all others.

                Also, the tradition of releasing a prisoner at Passover – ask a Jewish scholar about that one. It is an invention.

                Embroidery like this was all easy to do after the destruction of Jerusalem and the marginalization of Jews in the Empire.

                There was no universal census. If there had been one, it would have been for purpose of recording property for the purpose of taxation. So it would have been insane to require Joseph to leave the place where his goods were kept in order to register at the place of his ancestors 40 generations earlier. Also, he had to go through what was in effect enemy territory to get there. So why take his heavily pregnant wife?

                And finally: there were many, many chroniclers of the age, many of them interested in religion, two of them – Philo and Josephus – specifically interested in Jews and Jerusalem. How could Jesus the rock star of the bible have escaped their notice. And the NT says upon his death tombs opened and dead people rose and walk around the city. I think someone would have written that down.
