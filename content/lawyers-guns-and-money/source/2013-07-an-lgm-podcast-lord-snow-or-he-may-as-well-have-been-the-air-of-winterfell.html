      An LG&M podcast: “Lord Snow,” or “He may as well have been the air of Winterfell”

      [ 16 ] July 25, 2013 | SEK

      Steven Attewell and I decided that we didn’t want to wait until next February to continue talking about Game of Thrones, and so we decided to start over. Here’s our take on “Lord Snow,” the series’ third episode. And before you ask: yes, the podcast did explode before we had a chance to finish it. We’ll cover the four minutes we lost in two weeks, after I’ve moved and settled in. Which also means, obviously, that there won’t be a podcast next week, as I’ll be moving and settling in. Try not to miss me too much.

      Works SEK discusses:

      • “Lord Snow,” you’re no bigger than a half-man.

      Works Attewell discusses (warning, all of these posts contain spoilers for all five books):

      • Daenerys III (on assimilation and how Viserys fails to use its power)
      • Bran IV (old Nan’s stories and the meta-history of Westeros)
      • Eddard IV (Eddard’s first Small Council meeting, first assessment of his political skills, and the Littlefinger embezzlement question)
      • Catelyn IV (the knife, Littlefinger the gambler, his historical counterpart, and how he contrasts with Varys)
      • Jon III (the Watch as an institution in decline, Jon Snow getting over his privilege)
      • Arya II (Arya as a deconstruction of the fantasy heroic protagonist, Syrio Forel and famous swordswomen)

        To a certain extent, there’s a logical reason for class to be an important factor in personel management at The Wall. There are reasons they get put in charge.

        First, guys from noble houses come to the wall having basically completed the Westeros equivalent of the ROTC course. They (1) know how to read; (2) have weapons training, if not combat experience; (3) some tactical knowledge; (4) maybe some diplomatic observation; and (5) command experience, being used to living in fancy houses with servants and peasants that will do whatever they say. Command comes naturally to them. Not to mention they’ve been better fed and are consequently bigger and stronger than your average peasant.

        Second, The Wall has a real need for men. By treating lordlings and exiled knights well, they maintain positive relationships with the noble houses, and The Wall is retained as a viable option for their extra children and whatever criminals they have to bring to justice.

        Third, and this is not so much a great reason, but the Wall tends to be led by former lords who are more comfortable with other lords.

        These factors also play a role in Jon’s elevation. Jon was taught by Maester Luwin; was trained in weapons by Ser Rodrik; learned tactics and diplomacy from Ned Stark; and was raised in a castle at Winterfell. The Starks are also probably the most valuable house to the Wall – they take it more seriously than other great houses, and Ned is Hand of the King. That’s why he was groomed by Mormont. And it’s a big factor in why Sam is able to convince Cotter Pyke and Denys Mallister to go along with it.

Given how small the Night’s Watch is and how much it relies on superior discipline to win in battle, quality of leadership is crucial.

            • rw970 says:
              July 25, 2013 at 7:41 pm

              The question you ask about electoral democracy is interesting. Obviously, the Night’s Watch isn’t really a fraternal democracy. No military order could be. Even in the famously informal Israeli army where subordinates address their superiors by first name (gasp), at the end of the day, there are still ranks and commands. The penalty for violating an order at the NW is still death. And the franchise, as it were, seems limited to basically just deciding who the new Lord Commander will be, which may only happen once every few decades. And even for that, I suspect the highborn wield a lot more soft power than the lowborn. They probably have a lot more influence than the common rabble in who even gets nominated. I wouldn’t be surprised if they control blocks of votes.

              I would guess the Night’s Watch has to strike a delicate balance. It’s not a popular destination, so recruitment is always prevalent in the mind of whoever leads the organization. On the one hand, this means taking care of nobles; on the other, there’s the idea that anyone at all can rise high in the Watch, even the lowborn. Part of the latter is building up the idea that the Watch is a fraternity of brothers; equals. Sure, you can’t have kids or own land, or move somewhere with a tolerable climate, but you can rub shoulders with the elite! They’ll call you brother! Some day, you may even find yourself giving them orders!

              In practice, of course, the game is still rigged in favor of the highborn. They’re going to show up bigger, stronger, better trained, better educated. They’ll have a huge leg up. It’s still vastly less rigged than Westerosi society at large, though, where the lowborn don’t really get to even play.

              I think the highborn also value the democratic components of the Watch, to an extent. By joining the NW, they’re giving up many of their rights, perhaps chiefly their right to exit. Seeing as they can’t vote with their feet, it should be important to them that they should be able to have a say in their commander. I think a lot of the mercenary groups in Essos have similar concepts, where there’s a certain degree of buy-in from the members of the organization. Brown Ben takes care of his commanders, and they take care of their deputies, etc.; the Golden Company has council meetings where policy is debated.

              Additionally, the highborn need to keep the lowborn happy with the promise of advancement and voice, if only to ensure there are enough commoners to do the grunt work around the Wall. Otherwise, there won’t be enough command positions to go around, and some of the highborn will have to go chop wood and shovel shit. Too many lowborn, though, and the appeal wears off for the highborn. It’s a balance.

      5. rw970 says:

        I think a lot of Ned’s problems probably stem from his overabundance of caution, if anything. I think Ned didn’t plan on being Hand for a long period of time – he planned on returning to Winterfell.

        Much of the reason he took the job in the first place was to solve the murder of Jon Arryn, which turns into uncovering the Massive Conspiracy so Dark and Terrible that Doesn’t Really Exist At Least Not in The Way You Think It Does. Really, his goal is to find out who killed Jon Arryn, and bring them to justice (removing the perceived threat to his sister-in-law’s family); marry off Sansa to Joffrey (cementing his family’s position); and clear out some of the rot and decay in King’s Landing (which means taking the Lannisters, a family he doesn’t like, down a peg). After that, he’ll sail off into the sunset on the SS Live Forever, visit the Wall and meet up with Jon, and make more babies with Catelyn.

        The problem is that those are 3 very delicate tasks he’s set for himself, and many of them are in tension with each other. The oeverabundance of caution comes in here, as he thinks the Lannisters are involved in some incredible conspiracy and essentially run the country. He also believes, thanks to Littlefinger, that he’s being watched 24/7 by the Lannisters, which hamstrings his investigation. He’s also suspicious of how much his bond to Robert still really means, when the shit hits the fan. And, in fairness, he has reason to. Robert lets Cersei force Ned into killing Lady; refuses to punish Jaime Lannister for attacking the Hand and killing his guards; commands him to release Tyrion, who he thinks tried to kill his son; names Jaime Warden of the East; agrees to foster Robert Arryn with Tywin Lannister; and doesn’t heed his counsel regarding the Targaryens. Not to mention that Robert has also physically changed – he’s now fat and lazy. Who knows what else changed?

        Ned also assumes that everyone in KL is hostile to him (they probably are). He correctly adduces that Pycelle is a Lannister plant, that Varys can’t be trusted, and that Stannis and Renly have their own things going on. This causes him to overly rely on the only one even pretending to help him – Littlefinger. This is a huge mistake, but it’s not as if we know that Ned would have done better relying on Varys. There are no real good options here.
        But if Ned had realized the inherent power of his position, and that he was actually more influential with the King then anyone else, he would have been better equipped to take a stronger, more direct path to the resolution of the various goals he set for himself.
