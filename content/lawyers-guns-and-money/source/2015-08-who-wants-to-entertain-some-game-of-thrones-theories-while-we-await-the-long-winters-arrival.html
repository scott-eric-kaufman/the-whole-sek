        • Abigail Nussbaum says:
          August 29, 2015 at 4:37 am

          (c) reminds me a lot of what was going on in Harry Potter fandom during the wait for the later books.* The earlier books did a lot of neat foreshadowing stuff, particularly for the revelations that cap off Harry Potter and the Prisoner of Azkaban, which came as quite a surprise. But during the longer wait for the later books, everyone pretty much guessed the twists to come (mostly this was stuff having to do with Snape, but also things like Regulus Black). And because fandom is fandom, and because it had got it in its head that Rowling was a twisty, mischievous author rather than a normally clever one, you’d get people insisting with absolute certainty that NO WAY would Rowling do something as obvious as make Snape the person who told Voldemort about the prophecy that Harry would kill him; OBVIOUSLY it was a time-traveling Peter Pettigrew pollijuiced to look like Barty Crouch! You’re just falling for J.K.’s misdirections!

          Of course, in the end it turned out that the most obvious guesses were the correct ones, and that this was largely to the good, because those guesses didn’t just build off the hints planted in the text, but were true to the series’s themes and general thrust. Does Meera being Jon’s twin serve any sort of purpose, aside from making the theorizer feel clever (which is potentially fun in itself, of course)? I don’t think it does, so it’s probably not true.

          (* I should say that in general I see a lot of similarities between HP and ASOIAF. The latter is, of course, a more brutal story with a broader scope and much more elaborate history, but the basic concept, of young people being launched into a story that started many years before their birth, and being forced to figure it out even as they play a central role in it, is the same, as is the use of carefully planted plot twists and somewhat heartless willingness to kill beloved characters. Of course the most crucial difference is that Rowling managed to get to her planned ending with only a moderate amount of bloat and infelicities of plot, and I really don’t think that Martin is going to pull that off.)
