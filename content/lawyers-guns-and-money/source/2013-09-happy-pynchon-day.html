                • Anderson says:
                  September 17, 2013 at 8:20 pm

                  Not a quality unique to Pynchon: Bourget on decadence, over a century ago: “the unity of the book decomposes to give way to the independence of the page, where the page decomposes to give way to the independence of the sentence, and the sentence decomposes to give way to the independence of the word.”

                  Pynchon is acutely aware of that modern anxiety, which he addresses by paranoia: the reader of a book has the same conviction that it all fits somehow, that separate things are connected, that the paranoid has about reality. The catch is that the decadent is supposedly sick, but who’s sick really: the decadent who focuses on individual elements for what they are, or the paranoid who tries to make them fit? Is it necessary, if we are to be readers, to feign madness?
