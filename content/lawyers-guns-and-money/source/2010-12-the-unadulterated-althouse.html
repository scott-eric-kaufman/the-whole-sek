                    If “begs the question” (meaning “begs that the question be asked”) is “utterly superfluous” because we can just say “raises the question” instead, then “begs the question” (meaning “assumes the intended conclusion as a premise”) is also utterly superfluous, because we can just say “petitio principii“, or in fact “assumes the intended conclusion as a premise”, or “reasons in a circular fashion”.

                • The Horrible, but Nonethless Full of Year-End Good-Will Towards Mankind, Illegitimate Love-Child of Jenna Jameson and Henry W. Fowler says:
                  December 30, 2010 at 6:24 pm

                  Unlikely that SeanH, or indeed anybody else, is still following the thread at this late stage. But I saw this today and thought it’s something we can all agree on.

                  Anyway, SeanH, I am not a prescriptivist, or at least no more a prescriptivist than was King Canute.

                  It’s more a question of aesthetics, of style. Meaning and usage slip and change and there is nothing we can do about it. There is almost nothing we should do about it.

                  Save this: where there is a useful distinction or a well-established meaning, we should not lightly toss it overboard. “To beg the question” is not merely a philosopher’s terminus technicus. It has a meaning useful in ordinary conversation and writing. That meaning is watered down every time somebody uses the term to say something different.

                  Now, there is nothing morally (or socially! I really am not a snob, you know) wrong with using the term in that different way. Lots of people do so. The newer meaning is already well on its way to displacing the older, and one day no doubt it shall have triumphed altogether.

                  And that’s well and good. Language is a river, and as Heraclitus taught us, we can’t stand in it twice. But as against Cratylus I hope we may at least be allowed to stand in it once. The original meaning of “T.b.t.q” might be — almost certainly is — moribund. But we need not speed it into its grave.
