      I will make you love me.

      [ 28 ] January 3, 2011 | SEK

      The first fifteen minutes of Fight Club strongly suggest to its audience that they’re about to watch a much better film than the one they’re actually watching—the critique of material culture has yet to embrace to the juvenile punk resistence of the novel and the depiction of insomnia is still pointed enough to be trenchant—so when I decided to teach it in my “Confessional Narratives” course, focusing almost entirely on the opening sequences was essentially a given.  The problem is that David Fincher’s direction is as busy as it is effective, if only because representing a state of fevered distraction tends to look a bit frenetic.  (By my count, there are 659 cuts in the portion of the film I’m going to focus on, which means that any close reading of it will either be abridged or incomplete, depending on how trustworthy you think me.)  I’ll attempt to discuss the pace of the editing shortly, but for now I want to discuss why we even care.

      There are two lines of thought being represented in the first fifteen minutes of the film: the first is that the audience is being introduced to an unlikeable sociopath; the second is that the selfsame audience is being convinced to like him.  How does that work?  Via relentless frontality: when the audience stares into the void behind Edward Norton’s eyes long enough, the void stares back until it becomes a welcoming presence.  To wit:





      Note that the fourth wall is never actually being broken here: in the first image Norton is staring into space; the second, at his boss; the third, at a list of group meetings; and the fourth, into space again.  I point this out because frontality conventionally works to distance the audience from the characters by pulling the characters out of the diegetic world of the film and into that of the audience.  Here, however, Fincher is doing the exact opposite: he’s enforcing an intimacy between the audience and a character it might not otherwise sympathize with (and rightly so).  The audience identifies with Norton because it has stared into the void of his eyes and recognizes in them something inelegant and desperately wanting; and it does so because Fincher has provided them with no other choice.  The film simply doesn’t work if the sympathetic identification isn’t immediately established.

      Hence, the frontality.

        • C.S. says:

          I always thought of the book as being the shallow “joy of tearing shit down” member of the team. The film at least has the benefit of good actors to convey things which the book either gives short shrift to or else leaves out entirely — like the fact that you don’t really “like” the Narrator in the book, you’re just stuck with him and the book hopes that you have the same destructive impulse he does. Which is a problem because when you like the guy, you’re more implicated and invested in the bad shit he does. Its a richer experience for the moviegoer because of the internal conflict it sets up. The book doesn’t do that, to my mind.

          Larry McMurtry once said something to the effect that his reputation as a writer was helped considerably by the fact that the films made of his books (like Hud, Last Picture Show, and Terms of Endearment) were better than the books themselves — implying that people are so conditioned to viewing the book as the superior work of art that they retroactively gave him credit which he felt he didn’t earn. So I think the same is true here. If you see the movie first, you’re actually going to read more into the book than is there, and probably have a richer experience for it.

        This entire thread is insane. Fight Club is one of the masterpieces of world cinema. The list goes something like: La Règle du jeu, Apur Sansar, Fight Club.

        The movie is about masculinity and the appeal of fascism. The main character can’t express any emotion without pretending to be terminally ill. When this avenue is blocked for him — by a woman — he creates a hypermasculine world in which the only means of expression is through hitting and being hit. The result of this hypermasculinity is only destruction. The only (non-imaginary) character in the movie who has a name pursues extreme masculinity as a body builder. The pursuit itself unmans him to the point he loses his testicles, and gets “bitch tits”. He joins the fight club to get that masculinity back, and then ends up dead. The only time the main character feels at peace is when he shoots himself in the face.

        The movie is Susan Faludi’s Stiffed with added punching. Faludi argues that while a century ago men had a notion of masculinity built around doing things, the effect of advertising is to displace that with instead a masculinity of style — what Faludi terms “ornamental manhood”. The main character feels unsatisfied by mere consumption, and instead creates the perfect imaginary ornamental man.
