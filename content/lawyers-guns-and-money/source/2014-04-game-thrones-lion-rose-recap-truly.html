      7. Murc says:
        April 13, 2014 at 11:58 pm

        The most interesting part for me is some of the changes they’ve made from the books, and the reasons and consequences thereof. For example, the whole thing with Ramsay sending his hounds to hunt down ladies, that’s not ever shown in the books, only relayed secondhand from Reek. It’s kind of cool to see it.

        It’s also fascinating to see Jaime and Tyrion’s big old pity party. In the novels, Jaime and Brienne don’t make it back to King’s Landing until well after Tyrion’s show trial is underway. Jaime and Tyrion, at that point, actually hadn’t seen each other in person ever since Winterfell, way way way back at the beginning of the series, and when they do see each other again, Tyrion mocks Jaime and takes credit for murdering his kid, before indulging in some patricide. Here, the showrunners have taken the opportunity to put them together and get some compare-and-contrast going on.

        Jaime deciding to train with Bronn is also… interesting. And possibly dubious. Novel-Jaime is very, very serious about keeping the exact extent of his suckitude secret, because he would prefer some idiot like Boros Blount to not carve him apart. He trains in secret, at night, with either people he trusts deeply (Ser Addam Marbrand) or people who literally can’t talk (Ser Ilyn Payne.) But Bronn? Bronn is scum. (It’s implied in the novels that he’s also one of the very best swordsman, both intellectually and physically, in the world, but.) Bronn is the dude Tyrion pays to murder people for him and to pretend to be his friend. You don’t let people like Bronn see that you are weak.

        Oh, and as for SEK trying to make me feel guilty about cheering when Joffrey died… meh. If Joffrey were to be somehow held accountable for his many crimes, I would be against him dying, but only because I distrust that we can construct institutions responsible enough to fuck that sort of thing up. In a situation in which we can have sure and exact knowledge of exactly how sociopath the little weasel is, which can’t exist in real life but can in fiction, I got no problems standing up and cheering.

        I did feel somewhat dubious about being elated about the death of Book-Joffrey, who was fourteen when he died. TV-Joffrey is eighteen. That’s a lot different.
