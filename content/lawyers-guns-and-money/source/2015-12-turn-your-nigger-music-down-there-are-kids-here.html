      Another Day in the Life

      [ 413 ] December 9, 2015 | SEK

      (SEK is on line at the gas station waiting for a pump to open, listening to the “Hamilton” soundtrack, admittedly too loudly because he is deaf, when OLD WHITE MAN ambles up and taps on his window.)

      OLD WHITE MAN: (angrily) …!

      SEK: (rolls down window) Sorry, what, I couldn’t hear you?

      OLD WHITE MAN: (irately) …!

      SEK: (turns down “Hamilton” soundtrack) Sorry, again, what can I do you for?

      OLD WHITE MAN: Turn that crap down.

      “HAMILTON” SOUNDTRACK:
      Thomas, that was a real nice declaration,
      Welcome to the present, we’re running a real nation.
      Would you like to join us, or stay mellow,
      Doin’ whatever the hell it is you do in Monticello?

      SEK: What?

      OLD WHITE MAN: Turn your nigger music down, there are kids here.

      SEK: …‽

      OLD WHITE MAN: This used to be a Christian nation.

      “HAMILTON” SOUNDTRACK:
      A civics lesson from a slaver? Hey neighbor,
      Your debts are paid cuz you don’t pay for labor.
      “We plant seeds in the South. We create,”
      Yeah, keep ranting, we know who’s really doing the planting.

      (OLD WHITE MAN walks back to his car. SEK attempts to pick up his jaw from the floor mats of his.)

      SEK: (to himself) WHAT. THE. FUCK.

                  Not what I’m claiming.

                  The value of “learning from history” entails, to my mind, reconstructing the foundations of the present. History tells us how things got the way they are, and that is supremely valuable in formulating what comes after, but it in no way does that work all by itself. That those who don’t learn from history seemed doomed to repeat it is not because history is unchanging and/or cyclical, but because they have no clue where they are, and thus can’t determine where they need to go. It’s not surprising that a house built without a foundation will soon collapse in on itself.

                  What I’m arguing is that, to continue with Hamilton as an example, Hamilton has nothing useful to say about the present because he isn’t in the present. Even if we could draw reasonable conclusions about what he would think about any given present controversy, he will neither bear the responsibility of implementing his supposed recommendations nor be accountable if those recommendations don’t produce the intended result. And I think the kind of presentism that would point to him as a source for any kind of solution is designed to do exactly that, because it works as a kind of appeal to authority; it helps us evade responsibility and accountability for the decisions we make in the here and now, because we get to attribute those things to someone we think of as wise but who is ultimately too stone fucking dead to protest.

            This is an interesting example of what may already have a name, but what I think of as the “ecological argument,” which is used in normative or quasi-normative contexts, which is basically to say “You could be right, or just have a good case at being right, but it might not be better for everyone to agree with you if having a mix of different views, or just available spaces where your views aren’t dominant, is better for everyone.”

            I think it’s a really fascinating argument, but it’s actually pretty weak logically. After all, if you’re conceding the other person might be right, how much *more* right do they have to be before the value of toleration is swamped by the value of having everyone not be a terrible?

            As an example, if I made this argument on a debate about jokes built on offensive racial stereotypes – “well, I’m never going to say it in mixed company, it’s just for all of us non-race-members to have a giggle!” someone might justifiably respond that such things rarely stay walled in, they serve to perpetuate racist views that find much more dangerous and harmful ways of expressing themselves than just jokes, and like it or not stereotypes inform how we act in real situations. It’s clear that in some cases the ecological objection is very persuasive, too, like “people can have different norms of table manners at football games than at fancy dinner parties.” But in most of the intermediate cases, the argument is pretty vague and says more about the relative priorities of the arguers than any meaningful normative conclusions. Which is just to say, it has very little logical or normative force.
