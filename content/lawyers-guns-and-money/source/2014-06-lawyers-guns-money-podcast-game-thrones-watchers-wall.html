      4. Medicine Man says:
        June 24, 2014 at 3:44 pm

        A more interesting Podcast than I was anticipating, given the topic. The Podcast was almost entirely devoted to Scott and Steven arguing over what was wrong (or wasn’t) about the episode when usually there is more consensus. Also, somewhat uncharacteristically, Scott seemed to be having trouble articulating what bothered him about the episode, as Steven kept pointing out.

        Fair enough, I suppose. I know I had some trouble putting my finger on what was “off” about The Watchers on The Wall. I didn’t hate the episode, it just didn’t feel like all it could have been. That said I do think Scott is over thinking things.

        My opinion is that the episode had two primary problems:

        The first is that the resolution was pushed into the next episode. So despite a whole lot happening in the episode, it did very little to move the larger plot. That right there creates a lot of dissatisfaction.

        The second is that a lot of the things that happened were war movie cliches (as Scott mentions towards the end of the podcast). This is something Joe from Lowell mentioned in the previous thread about this episode: Watchers on the Wall really took shelter under the macho-man canards common to war flicks. At the time I disagreed, feeling that the story was more about Westeros’ castoffs anonymously fighting and dying doing critically important shit work no one “important” could be bothered to take care of; call it the “Dirty Dozen” aspect of the Night Watch. Upon reflection, I think Joe is more right than I am. The bastards in battle aspect of the episode is subtext and the nobility in the fires of battle is text.

        Overall, I think this is a wasted opportunity. It would have been a lot more interesting if, instead of uttering strained bravado while ordering yet another arrow volley, Ed had raved about their shared frustration as downtrodden, cast aside men and how they weren’t going to get walked over no matter how many wildlings came. Similarly, before starting his final mantra, they should have Gren interrogate the one waverer — “You’re really going to leave us here to fight without you?” Really drive home the point that while any of the Night Watch could have run, doing so would be leaving behind the only people left on Westeros who were still on their side. I thought Sam’s parts in this episode worked well but most of the rest wasn’t quite up to the same level. Compare and contrast the 11th hour speeches in this episode to what Tyrion tells the defenders before they sally out to destroy the battering ram and the difference in quality becomes more clear.

        • gmack says:
          June 25, 2014 at 2:07 pm

          Agreed, in general. I’m definitely with SEK on his assessment of this as a stand-alone episode. The battle was competently done, but just didn’t seem that interesting. But then again, I’m nearly alone in thinking the same thing about “Blackwater,” which I disliked even more (after the end of the second season, I stopped watching the show until about a month ago, when for some reason I decided to catch up). Broadly speaking, I think the discussion here points to something I find off about the show in general: It’s not really about anything in particular. SEK insists that the events at the Wall are in fact the most important story in the show and that in fact the dynastic maneuverings are just distractions. This is accurate, so far as I can tell (I haven’t read the books), but to my mind points to pretty significant weaknesses in story telling. If the main issue at stake here is the threat coming from the north, then why not try to emphasize this? Why not tell the story in a way that shows the foolishness of “the game”? Beyond that, it seems to me that the the constantly expanding stories combined with the tendency to kill off (or radically transform) many of the characters the audience identifies with means that there is a real tendency of these stories to veer toward nihilism: the events become a jumble of increasingly horrible but ultimately meaningless happenings. I should add that I find the show entertaining–I’m still watching it, after all–but I have some pretty serious misgivings about the whole thing.

      10. Cheap Wino says:
        June 30, 2014 at 11:07 am

        Finally got a chance to listen to this.

        I’d agree with Scott, that the episode on the whole was kind of ho-hum, though there were some high points — Allister Thorne’s showing as a truly worthy character when it comes to battle got it exactly right; same with Janos Slynt’s cowardice.

        I’d go even further than Scott in my assessment of Ygritte’s death. Not only was “you know nothing” tiresomely predictable but the whole idea that she died in his arms was much, much too Hollywood. Throw in the also predictable bit about the killing blow coming from the boy who’s parents she killed and it really comes off campy.

        A major part of the appeal of the presentation, both book and show, is the appreciation of the nasty, brutish and shortness of life. We’ve been accustomed to unexpected death — think of the reactions of people to Oberyn Martell’s demise in the previous episode. For Ygritte’s death to happen so melodramatically, in the midst of a battle no less, brought a cheese factor that flat out did not work in the context of the story and what we’ve become accustomed to.

        I was ready for it to happen differently than in the book (where I think GRRM got it right), but was disappointed at how un-GOT-like it was.
