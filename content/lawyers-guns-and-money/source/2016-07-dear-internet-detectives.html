      Dear Internet Detectives,

      [ 156 ] July 6, 2016 | SEK

      Please just stop.Â It’s abundantly clear where my sympathies rest on this issue, but everyone needs to stop scrutinizing low-quality cell-phone videos and determining, definitively-to-their-mind, whether Alton Sterling did or did not have a gun.

      The problems are larger than this incident and whether he had a gun or was only thought to by the officers — the problems are systemic. If he turns out to have had a gun, conservatives will crow about “the convicted sex offender who resisted arrest while armed and got what he deserved,” and dismiss the need for any larger issue to be addressed.

      And then we lose.

      So stop playing Internet Detective with insufficient evidence and base your argument on the fact that because Sterling was SUSPECTED of having a gun, he was slammed to the ground, held down, and shot dead — whereas Dylann Roof, the Charleston church shooter, was KNOWN to have murdered multiple innocent parishioners and pulled over, escorted to Burger King in a bullet-proof vest, and is currently receiving the due process supposedly guaranteed to every American.

                • americanpride says:
                  July 7, 2016 at 9:54 am

                  There are two different issues being conflated here.

                  First – probable cause is not the standard for making a stop or temporary detention. Reasonable suspicion (Terry v. Ohio) is the standard for making a temporary detention. Reasonable suspicion is based on the totality of the circumstances as they were known at the time of the stop, which in this case includes the anonymous tip describing a person matching Sterling’s description brandishing a weapon. Probable cause enters the equation when the officers decide to make an arrest and conduct a search. Additionally, officers have the authority to use the minimum force necessary to effect a lawful detention or lawful arrest. Whether or not Sterling had a weapon is actually less important than whether the officers had a reasonable belief, based on the totality of circumstances, he had a weapon and had the intention to use it.

                  Second – that said, had Sterling been lawfully detained and arrested, felon in possession of a firearm is a federal offense that carries up to 10 years in prison. Aggravating circumstances increase it to 15 years. That’s if it the AUSA decides to pursue it. State penalties vary.

                  The frustration many people have with these kinds of incidents is not just rooted in perceptions of racism (which in many cases are legitimate). It’s also in part caused by the low bar needed for officers to employ deadly force (a reasonable belief there is a risk of death or serious physical harm). This legal bar is actually lower than what many police departments accept. Many police departments policies explicitly state that use of deadly force is a last resort but the law does not actually require this. On an interesting note – a study found that the more highly educated a police officer, (1) the less likely they were to use deadly force and (2) when they did use deadly force, they more likely that it was justified. Of course, the requirements for police recruits are fairly low.
