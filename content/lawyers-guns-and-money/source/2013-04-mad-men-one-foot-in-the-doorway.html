            • SEK says:
              April 9, 2013 at 6:45 am

              His point is that Hawaii and Vietnam have the same tropical climate and atmosphere, and mentions that the hotel employees look like the enemy.

              I didn’t get to this in this post, but I think his point was also that Korea wasn’t nearly as deadly as the Vietnam — about half as many dead and wounded — and that Dinkins was making a special pleading to Don, on behalf of having to fight in a more hellish war, to give away his bride.

              As for the two-shots and reaction shots, I didn’t note them because they’re par for the course: the two-shot establishes they have some connection, but the reaction shots indicate whose reaction we’re supposed to value and/or be invested in. It’s a solid observation, and one I should’ve made, if only because it makes the episode even more about Don: even when he’s agreeing to give away a stranger to a stranger, it’s more about him than either of them, at least according to the camera.

              Don may not be thinking of his own death.

              I didn’t press the anhedonia angle because I needed to establish his general malaise first, but the way the episode’s structured, with a Rosen-saved life and Rosen-skiing-off-to-save-another, makes for a death-related bookend that Don’s trying to understand, but seems unable to connect with. He may not be thinking about his own death, but he’s certainly concerned that he doesn’t care about it anymore. In the previous season premiere, he was in a doctor’s office being told how to live a healthy, long life; here, he’s in his lobby watching, helplessly — and visibly disturbed — as Rosen huffs the doorman back to life. The look on his face, and the cuts to the medium close-ups in a scene that really shouldn’t have been about him, indicate that he feels in the presence of two miracles: the first is that the doorman can be revived, the second that someone would even bother to revive him.
