      SEK’s Game of Thrones recap: “The Mountain and The Viper”

      [ 115 ] June 2, 2014 | SEK

       

      This week’s recap comes complete with necessary references to both My Girl and Apocalypse Now. Something to whet your appetite:

      A key aspect of this scene is that Graves lets the camera linger on both Missandei and Grey Worm for a few beats too long before reversing the shot, which creates an odd effect. Instead of seeing how the other character is reacting to what each of them says — which is what typically happens with a reverse shot, as Person A says something and the camera cuts to Person B reacting to it — Graves allows the audience to see both Missandei and Grey Worm reacting to what they’ve just said, which calls to mind the self-conscious awkwardness of a first date. They’re both judging themselves before they give the other a chance to, and it’s charming, because they’re both far more forgiving of the other’s missteps than they are of their own.

          • Murc says:
            June 2, 2014 at 10:04 am

            Also too, Oberyn wasn’t going into battle. He was going into a one-on-one duel. Those are massively different things.

            In single combat, speed and pure skill with a blade are what’s going to bring you out of it alive, not being a walking tank. Syrio Forel, a professional duelist, didn’t wear armor, because armor would have slowed him down and a quicker bravo would have darted past his guard and slid his rapier into his eye or into any one of a number other gaps in the armor. It also tires you out, wears you down faster. That’s one of the major reasons Bronn is alive and Ser Vardis Egan is not.

            Syrio also didn’t carry a big, clumsy blade designed to cut through armor; that might be useful on the battlefield, but in single combat you can pick your shot and you need to be able to strike and then recover fast.

            This actually parallels, to an extent, the evolution of blades and bladework here in the real world. Your classic light blades from the High Middle Ages were developed primarily to be used by young men who’d been well-trained who would be fighting other well-trained young men one on one, possibly restrained by a dueling code, possibly not.

            There’s a scene in “Romeo and Juliet” where Lord Capulet calls for his “long sword.” (Two words in most editions, not just one.) That’s actually thought by many to be a cultural signifier of Lord Capulet being old-school and a bit out of touch, carrying the implement he would have wielded in battle as a young man into an inappropriate situation as an older one.
