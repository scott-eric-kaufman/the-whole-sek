      R.I.P. Iain M. Banks

      [ 40 ] June 9, 2013 | SEK

      Not that it’s unexpected, but all signs point to Iain M. Banks having lost his battle with cancer this morning. I am, for the record, allowed to mourn his passing without conservative self-promoters like John Ziegler attacking me for falsely lionizing the recently deceased. Should he or any like-minded asshole try any such thing, I have no doubt he’ll feel the full force of the online equivalent of Special Circumstances.

      I’ll write a Banks a proper send-off once I gather my thoughts, but for now, I’ll acknowledge we’ve lost a great one and leave it at that.

        My encounters with Banks have been of a far more common type than Brimmicombe-Wood’s: I read his books, and enjoyed them greatly.

        I especially liked a feature of several of his books that it seems you rarely find in Science Fiction: a story that centers on the fate of a character – not the fate of the Universe. Even when a character is convinced the fate of the entire world hinges on their actions, even when the reader is convinced by the character’s belief, they’re often wrong: machinations they’re unaware of have already rendered their efforts moot, or their goal was just not that important to begin with, really. It’s a useful antidote to the grandiosity lazily used by so many other genre authors.

        • Doctor Memory says:
          June 9, 2013 at 5:18 pm

          Start with “The Player of Games” or “Use of Weapons”, which I think are the best introductions to the Culture universe. Then circle back around to “Consider Phelbas”: it was his first attempt at a big SF novel, and a lot of the rough edges show. You could safely ignore it entirely except that it sets up a lot of the backstory for “Look to Windward” which for my money is his absolute masterpiece.

          After that, the order hardly matters much, and the quality varies a bit. (Which is say, between “thoroughly entertaining” to “amazing” — “Excession” is the closest thing to a mediocre novel in the bunch and even it has its moments.) There’s a very, very vague chronology of the Culture universe that winds through the books, but you don’t have to pay much attention to it: Windward and “Surface Detail” are the only ones with explicit callbacks to earlier novels.
