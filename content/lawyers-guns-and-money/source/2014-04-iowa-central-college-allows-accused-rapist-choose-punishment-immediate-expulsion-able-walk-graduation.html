            April 30, 2014 at 10:56 pm

            I think it probably depends on what discipline is at stake

            This, I think, is why you often see the preponderance standard used as the default in university disciplinary proceedings (and I’ll be speaking here in the general sense, not specifically about sexual assault cases). Generally, the purpose of the sanctions for students found responsible is educational, rather than punitive (although that doesn’t mean that the students don’t experience the sanctions as punitive, at least at first) – disciplinary probation (i.e., keep your nose clean for the next semester/year/til graduation or we’ll go harder on you next time); educational programs geared to the type of violation; restitution where appropriate, like in cases of vandalism, etc.

            As you note, expulsion is in a different league from those types of sanctions, even suspension, in terms of its effect on the accused student, which does raise the question of whether the preponderance standard is appropriate. I think one difficulty with raising the standard is that the determination of responsibility is often done before the decision on a possible sanction is made (heck, at my institution, we don’t even open the envelope with info on possible past violations of the code of conduct until *after* the board has decided that the student is responsible for the current charge; we don’t even know if the student is currently on probation til then).

            This means that in some cases where expulsion might be on the table (repeated violations, for instance), you’ve got a procedural catch-22: if the student’s past history makes expulsion a possibility, you’ve just found him/her responsible under the preponderance standard – oops! However, if all *possible* expulsion cases get a higher standard of proof, the board would have to be told in advance about the prior violations, which could bias their judgment of the current case. Admittedly, such instances are probably extremely rare.

            Of course, there are going to be a handful of violations where expulsion is a possibility even if the accused student has never been in trouble before (like rape, or other types of violence; at some institutions, serious drug violations can be enough, I think). I can see an argument for a higher standard of proof in those cases; however, the counter-argument I would raise is that this does introduce a situation where we’re using a higher standard of proof for rape cases and very little else, which is…problematic, to say the least. (Not to mention the fact that at least some rape cases will be of the sort where the evidence supports a “responsible” verdict under preponderance but not beyond reasonable doubt – and if we go with the latter standard, we wouldn’t be able to impose any sanction at all in those cases.)

            Apologies for the rambling…I’m sort of thinking “out loud” here.

        • Bloix says:
          April 30, 2014 at 1:30 pm

          Not obvious that the “preponderance of the evidence” standard should apply.

          “Preponderance” is for lawsuits to recover damages – losses that can quantified in money terms. You breached a contract with me and I am out of pocket $100,000. Or you ran me over and I can prove $100,000 in medical bills, lost wages, and pain and suffering.

          The idea is that there has been a monetary (or monetizable) loss, and someone has to bear it. Should it be me or you? If the evidence is 50% + 1 smidgen that you are at fault, the law says, it is “fair” for you to bear the loss. If the evidence is 50% – 1 smidgen, that you are at fault, then it is “fair” for me to bear it.

          This has no relationship to what a college disciplinary board is doing. The board is not deciding who must bear a loss. It is deciding whether it needs to protect one party and/or punish the other, and it should have flexibility in applying the standard of proof depending on what it needs to do.

          If the disciplinary board wants to do something that makes the complaining party (whether she can prove she’s a “victim” is not relevant) feel safer, but doesn’t inconvenience the accused party very much, then a very low standard of proof would make sense. An order saying no contact, a move to a different dorm, a requirement that he not take classes or belong to clubs or teams that she is in. For that, something like the “some credible evidence” standard would be sufficient proof.

          If the board wants to do something that helps the victim but does inconvenience the accused party substantially, then a preponderance standard would be reasonable. A one-year suspension in order to let the complaining party graduate or move them into different class years, for example.

          If the board wants to do something that is primarily punitive, like expulsion, then a higher standard – perhaps the”clear and convincing evidence” standard used in non-criminal cases that affect personal rights – would be appropriate.

          Under the present system, the woman cannot be protected without first finding that the man is “guilty.” That’s not working, for the accused or the accusers.
