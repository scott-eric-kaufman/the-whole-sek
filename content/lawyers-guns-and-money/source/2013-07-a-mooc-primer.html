      A MOOC Primer by SEK

      [ 43 ] July 21, 2013 | SEK

      Once upon a time I did one of these that proved so popular Insider Higher Ed promised to pay me $125 to reprint it at their place. And they did reprint it yes they did. But I digress: last night I was avoiding things I couldn’t change, but lacked the courage required to change the things I could, so instead I decided to write a primer summarizing my feelings about the MOOC phenomenon I’ve been reading so much about lately. It goes something like this:

      A is for affordable, you too can attend!

      B, the boredom of the automated emails that they send.

      C is for the crowd-sourced marks you never can protest,

      D, the comments you receive from Dr. Dispossessed.

      E is for efficiency, you’ll learn much more and faster;

      F, the mark you’ll earn from your anonymous headmaster.

      G is for goals you’ll meet, should fleeting time permit;

      H, the honey badger, who doesn’t give a shit.

      I‘s for innovation, as the Internet is used;

      J‘s the campus jobs “human appliances” will lose.

      K is for “the kids,” for whom all this is done;

      L, the learning process, endless lectures on re-run.

      M is for the money that will soon begin to rain,

      O for the obscenity of pipe dreams, most profane.

      O‘s also for objectives and assessments by machines,

      C, “collaboration,” ‘cross seas of dim-lit screens.

      Q is for the questions never answered in a MOOC,

      R for “really thoughtful answers” copied from a book.

      S, the rare impoverished souls, who care enough to try,

      T, the time spent wasted, “learning” from a turned blind eye.

      U‘s for universities, who seek to monetize,

      V, the vastness of the campus they’ve lobotomized.

      W‘s for the wallet, slit wide and fit to pad,

      X is the amount slipped in, the bills that feed the fad.

      Y is for the questions time and space do not permit,

      Z, the honey badger, who doesn’t give a shit.

      NOTE: Editorial advice is always welcome, as deaf scansion tends to emphasize rhythm at the expense of everything else.

        • StevenAttewell says:
          July 21, 2013 at 5:52 pm

          Except that MOOCs are being promoted as a solution to this, when:

          1. They’re unlikely to reduce the pressures on grade inflation; when education is further treated as a business which relies on mass consumption to thrive, you don’t piss off your customers by not giving them decent grades for their dollars.

          2. Plagiarism is going to be even harder to catch when you don’t have professors and TAs who have regular contact with students and learn their writing styles, and thus can spot plagiarism.

          3. There won’t be any more professors; just a few superstars who’ll make a temporary killing up until employers realize they can just can them and replace them with actors, and a huge underclass of poorly-paid adjuncts with ever-increasing workloads, and who’s going to spend years and years of their lives getting a PhD to do that?

          4. Lectures will still be boring, but now they’ll be video tapes instead of actually being in person. Which is soooo much better, because who goes to see live music when you have Youtube? /sarcasm.

          5. Cheating is only going to get easier; much of the savings from going to MOOC comes from re-using the same course materials year after year so that you don’t have to pay professors to redesign their courses. You’re going to find exams and answer guides all over the internet the moment anyone on the internet catches on.

          6. See #1.

          7. Speaking as a TA, we’re going to be screwed if MOOCs become dominant. Our hard-fought rights when it comes to workload protections (so we can both work and be graduate students) will be completely destroyed and the numbers of TA jobs (which is the majority of the way people pay for graduate school) will shrink to nothing, leaving massive debt the only option for the non-wealthy.

        • Lasker says:
          July 22, 2013 at 12:02 pm

          Interesting – I’m in the same two classes, though my impressions were a bit different.

          I’ve been enjoying the startup class very much, though probably not for exactly the reasons the teachers intended. As someone just getting started programming, the focus on workflow and all the little programs that help you get things done has been filling in a lot of gaps for me. The business stuff has been interesting more as a window into Silicon Valley ideology than as a recipe that I would ever actually use as my own ambitions are more in the small-business than the startup realm. Some of the “philosophy” portion is such a caricature of out-of-touch techno fetishism/neoliberalism I had trouble believing at first that it wasn’t tongue-in-cheek. Some lefty professors could register and really tear them a new one, although I suppose the teachers of the class would only see it as confirmation they are on the right track. Still, the nuts-and-bolts portion of the class has been very helpful to me.

          The linear algebra class on the other hand has been quite disappointing, for several reasons. The lectures are, IMO, quite poor. They might be effective for teaching someone who was reviewing the material, but move way too quickly for someone seeing the topic for the first time. Without the (promised) textbook, the course is also quite incomplete – the lecture slides just aren’t sufficient or organized enough to stand alone.
          There are also many small decisions in the course that don’t work well. For example, I appreciate the fact that we have such lenient deadlines, but this prevents people from discussing the answers to the questions until long after the class has moved on to the next topic. This is especially critical in any class involving programming, where at least half the learning happens when changing an ugly but workable solution to something more elegant – which is hard to do if you can’t share your code.

          I finally decided it was hopeless to wait for the course-specific text and ordered the Gilbert Strang book, which will hopefully help me get back on track. I would have dropped this course if the material wasn’t necessary for a personal project I’m working on.

          On MOOCs as a whole:

          I’ve taken part in at least a dozen MOOCs in the last year, and the two strongest were:

          Introduction to Interactive Programming in Python through Rice/Coursera

          Calculus One from Ohio State/Coursera

          with an honorable mention for MIT’s 6.00x, another intro to computer science through Python.

          I think the Rice python class and the Calculus class above would be good places to start for people who want to see how good MOOCs can be. From what I’ve seen, the majority of MOOCs out there are mediocre (and some are worse than that), but the best are very good indeed.

          My positive experience with MOOCs makes reading all the MOOC-hate from writers who I generally trust a strange experience. On the whole I think that MOOC detractors are a) 100% correct about the pernicious end-goals of the biggest MOOC boosters and b) significantly underestimating how educationally effective MOOCS can be.
