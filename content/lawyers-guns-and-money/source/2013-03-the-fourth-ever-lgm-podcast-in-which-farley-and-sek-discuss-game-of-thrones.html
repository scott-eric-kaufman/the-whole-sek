        * House of the Undying – yes it didn’t quite work, but I think there’s merit there. The writers said they had big problems with this storyline and here’s what I think they were: prophecies are tough in visual media, and the specific prophecies GRRM sets up are even more so. Unlike in a book where you can underline things and go back to them pretty easily, it’s more difficult to remember the couple of dozen multi-part prophecies Dany gets. Second, because they decided on a no-flashback policy, a bunch of the prophecies (I’m thinking of Rhaegar and the dragon has three heads) won’t resonate because the audience doesn’t know who they are. Third, either prophecies will be too abstract (the woman and the dwarfs, the flicker-flash of images with the mummer’s dragon and co) or too visually identifiable (Scarlet Rehearsal Dinner) and will spoil things. Fourth, assuming people remember the prophecies, there’s a danger they get so obsessed about them that we get a Lost-type situation where a vocal part of the fandom only cares about that and gets angry when they’re not fulfilled on time or in the way they imagined them to be.

        Some close readings of close readings (27:00 to end):

        * Regarding Robb Stark and his wife: they’re absolutely doing the Scarlet Rehearsal Dinner. However, I think they’ve changed the character of Jayne Westerling (which…ouch, SEK) into Talisa Maegyr for an entirely different reason.

        * I’m hoping that Farley was collapsing the aftermath of Joffrey’s wedding up to Tyrion’s travels into the wedding, because the events of the second half of ASOS are absolutely critical and amazing.

        * Arya = Batman. I’m surprised no one brought this up.

        * Generally surprised at your students’ preferences – an unscientific poll of tumblr and the interwebs more generally says that there is much love for Robb/Richard Madden from the portion of the fandom attracted to males. His and Catelyn’s storyline wasn’t well served by the editors, with changes in sequence really changing the meaning of both of their actions.

        * Regarding Jon Snow – I’m kind of surprised no one brought up how the changes from the book made him into a screwup throughout the season. In the book, Jon Snow is an exemplary ranger and a core part of Quorin Halfhand’s (another poorly used character)SEAL-esque team, who are undone by an imbalance of magical technology, not because Jon ran off after Ygritte.

        * I feel like you missed an obvious parallel. If Ser Barristan is Gandalf, Jorah Mormont is Boromir.

        * I like Dany as Grant quite a bit, because I see strong elements of Reconstruction in her narrative of failed state-building, and arguably Reconstruction was the U.S’ first failed attempt to rebuild a state after conquering it in a war.

        * re: Jon’s plot in Book 5. He’s got a lot of outs. Rh’llorist magic both in the strong form (Thoros and Beric) and the weak form (Moqorro and Victarion), the warging trick, etc.

        * Prophecy isn’t necessarily useless, it’s just that there are different kinds of prophecy. See here.

        * Ramsey – definitely worse than Joffrey. When it comes down to it, Joffrey’s weakness is that he’s a physical coward. He forces other people to do hideous things to people who can’t fight back, but he never has the gumption to get his own hands dirty. Ramsey is many things, but he’s not a coward, and if you fight back, he just hits you harder. I do think Theon will be gradually redeemed under the weight of his…ministrations, but it’s going to be rough going. Also, I have no idea how they’re going to do his storyline in Season 4 – they may need to do a Mormont-like break for a season.

        * Stannis’ Season 4 reveal is going to be awesome. I agree he needs to come forward as an un-charismatic hero marked by his absolute determination, think Oliver Cromwell/Ho Chi Minh/George Washington. His trek isn’t futile – if you want to understand the Pink Letter, read the preview chapter for The Winds of Winter.

        * I’m surprised that SEK didn’t appreciate the awesomeness that is Davos, the one working-class protagonist of the entire series – and it’s not an accident that Davos, like Ser Dunk the Tall, was born in the slums of Flea Bottom. Go back to his chapters and give them a re-read, but think of Davos as what Ned Stark would be if Jon Arryn hadn’t pushed honor before reason. If Brienne the Beauty is the one true knight in the series in part because of how she perseveres despite every handicap and all of her suffering, Davos is the one true lord in the series, a man who is willing to tell the hard truth and serve his liege lord at the cost of his own life, but who also works to hold his liege lord to account and make him someone worth following. When Davos stands up in the court in Book 5 and says “Ser Jared, I name you liar,” all right-thinking people should cheer.

        * Democracy is an interesting thing in ASOIAF.
