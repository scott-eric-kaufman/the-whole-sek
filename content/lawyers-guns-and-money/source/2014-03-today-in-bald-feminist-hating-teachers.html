              In the absence of any observable action taken agains the students or their message, the much more likely explanation is that the administration is responding to what they see as a story about an “edgy,” sexually-themed topic by worrying about “what’s next,” and implementing controls out of a concern that something they do find objectionable might be published in the future.

              It’s really not so different from how a teacher might remind the class of the raise-your-hand rule when a discussion gets into direct student back-and-forths, even when the discussion has been a good one. They do this not because they disapprove of the discussion that just happened, but to avoid the impression that the class is a free-for-all.

              I once subbed in a language-arts pullout classroom, in which the activity of the day was for the kids to play Scrabble. One of the kids got the letters for “Vagina.” He called me over and asked me if that would be an acceptable word to use, or if he would get in trouble. I told him, and his three opponents, and the rest of the class (as they pretended they weren’t listening) that, yes, that’s a perfectly legitimate word, one you might encounter in biology class, and to go ahead and use it. Then I added, “But you may not giggle about it. You may not sit here and have a conversation about. It’s a perfectly normal word, and you may play it and treat it as a perfectly normal word, like mature people.” They all listened very closely, to understand what the ruling was, and went on with the game.

              Why did I add that, if I didn’t have any problem with him playing the word? Because I didn’t want the class to turn into a free-for-all. If kids see someone “get away with something” that they think pushes a boundary, some of them will want to see how far they can push it. Some of them will think it means there are no more boundaries, so let’s cut loose. And something that deals with a sexual topic, being published in the school paper, is going to look to some kids like someone got away with pushing a boundary.

              Maybe it’s an overreaction – Willie, I want you to take all of the colored chalk out of the classrooms – but even if it is, both the impetus and the consequences seem to be much less sinister than they’re being made out to be.
