      The “moral panic” of tenured literature professors

      [ 30 ] April 9, 2014 | SEK

      I scored an interview with the author of the controversial Chronicle article, Marc Bousquet.* I tried to make the stakes of the debate clear to non-academic readers, and I think I succeeded.

      Feel free to tell me if I didn’t, and more importantly, why I didn’t. I’m in a bit of a forest-for-the-trees position when it comes to writing about academia. I know the landscape really well, but I’m not entirely sure you’d want me to draw you a map.

      *By “scored” I mean I asked him and he said, “Of course!”

        Hmmm….I greatly value Bosquet’s thinking aboutnthe academic labor system, but I wish he’d put this intra-literary debate in a little broader context. It reminds me of the state of Oklahoma Democratic Party politics when I arrived in the Sooner State in the late ’90s. The Democrats were still largely in charge of the state. Though the Governor was a Republican, most of the other elected state constitutional offices were Dems. Democrats controlled both houses of the legislature and had done so uninterrupted since the 1920s. But the writing was on the wall: the state voted solidly Republican in presidential elections, both US Senators were Republican, and all but one Congressperson was, too (and that seat was about to flip). And what were the Democrats doing? Arguing bitterly with themselves over a rural-urban split within the party that, at least to someone newly arrived in the state, seemed to be about very little of importance. Now Democrats control nothing in the state and the great rural-urban divide seems to no longer be worth anyone’s while to worry about.

        I wish Eng Lit scholars, humanists, and academics in general would show a little more solidarity at this moment. Whether we’re Ivy or state-school trained, whether we wish our students would read Shakespeare or become literate in the digital humanities, we all have a target on our backs right now. I suspect that in twenty years the argument Bosquet is worried about will seem as quaint as the old rural-urban Oklahoma Democratic divide.

      11. I'm Nobody -- what's it to you? says:
        April 9, 2014 at 7:44 pm

        I have a dog in this fight, friends. I teach lit in an English department. Our doctoral students get jobs when they do — and they do, though not as often as we’d like – because they are cross-trained in teaching writing and teaching lit (and often in teaching creative writing as well). Small liberal arts colleges hire our graduates because they have a variety of skills, not just one. And those skills do translate from class to class, and even from exam area to exam area — if we let them. My dog in this fight is this: students should get every single skill they can get. If your lit students don’t have comp-rhet training, they’re kind of screwed; if your comp-rhet students can’t teach the odd class in lit, they, too, are kind of screwed.

        Yeah, no, I don’t mean can’t we all get aloooong — except that I kind of do mean that. I went to grad school in the ’80s, at the height of the theory wars (see my scars!), so I suppose I am not at all interested in pitting different fields against each other. If we are going to make English a viable major / field / graduate program — insofar as that’s possible — it’s only if we broaden what counts as English / literary and cultural studies. Both professionally and intellectually.

        I taught a doctoral seminar last semester in American women novelists. Excellent papers from both a rhet-comp student and a creative writing student, whose novel will be her dissertation. And from the regular lit students. The multiplicity in the room was productive for all of us. I’m not in a panic, and people who are — are not paying attention.
