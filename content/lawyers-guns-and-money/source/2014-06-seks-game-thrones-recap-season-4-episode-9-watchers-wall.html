      3. Murc says:
        June 9, 2014 at 8:20 am

        “The Watchers on the Wall” felt oddly thin, like it was an excuse to have a battle, instead of a battle that came as the culmination of a season-long arc.

        The entire TV-show-only Night’s Watch plotline they’ve been using to pad out the season has felt like that. I honestly, truly think they fucked up the pacing leading up to this season such that their choices were either “very little Kit Harrington indeed” or “make some shit up for him to do.”

        I kind of wish they’d gone with the former, really.

        All these long and medium CGI shots are seemingly meant more to inspire admiration for their execution than what they should — namely, fear.

        There are also probably budgetary and technical limitations at work here.

        I call this Avengers Syndrome. The Avengers proved that modern CGI has really come into its own for rendering detailed, complex battle scenes… if you have, you know, a quarter of a billion dollars to throw at it. Which almost nobody else does, but now people expect everything to look just as good as the Chitauri did swooping down over Manhattan in the middle of the day.

        So film and television makers have to cheat. Lots of long and medium shots are a way of doing that, because you can massively scale down model detail. So is setting scenes at night, in the rain, in situations where the models are, say, waist-deep in water or obscured by smoke and weather. Because then you don’t have to render their entire body. Pacific Rim and Godzilla are great examples of this sort of thing.

        I’ve seen a lot of people complain that Daenerys’ dragons don’t look nearly was good as the White Walkers or the giants and mammoths. And, well… the dragons have to appear in broad daylight, surrounded by humans in colorful clothing in very real, exotic locales. Of course they don’t look as good as the White Walkers, who require only a limited color palette, usually at night with snow blowing around them, usually seen only with other “real” actors who are likewise wearing muted colors and not in full daylight.

        even that one memorable death — Ygritte’s — feels almost overburdened by the inevitably of her last words. That should feel like a moment, but by the time she finally informs Jon Snow that he knows nothing, it feels more like an empty punchline to a joke we’ve already heard.

        I don’t usually have much bad to say about the acting on GoT, but they really fucked this up. It felt like everyone involved was actively restraining themselves from winking at the camera during the leadup to Ygritte saying The Line. In the novel, Martin is sufficiently skilled to make us forget about the natural narrative conclusion to Ygritte taking a few arrows until she actually delivers her last line. They did not manage this in the show.
