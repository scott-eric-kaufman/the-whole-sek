      More further adventures of OLDMAN CAT

      [ 33 ] December 3, 2015 | SEK

      OLDMAN CAT: HOW CLEAN IS THIS WATER

      SEK: I just changed it.

      OLDMAN CAT: ON A SCALE OF 1 TO 10

      SEK: It’s a ten.

      OLDMAN CAT: IN WHICH 1 IS DIRTY AND 10 IS REALLY FUCKING DIRTY

      SEK: I just changed it.

      OLDMAN CAT: SO THAT’S A 1

      SEK: No, it’s a zero.

      OLDMAN CAT: OH YOU MEAN IT’S PRE-DIRTY

      SEK: No, I mean it’s clean.

      OLDMAN CAT: I DON’T KNOW I’M TESTING IT WITH MY PAW AND IT FEELS PRETTY PRE-DIRTY

      SEK: “Pre-dirty” means “clean,” same thing.

      OLDMAN CAT: NOW IT FEELS REALLY DIRTY

      SEK: Like someone got out of the litter box and stuck his paw in his water bowl?

      OLDMAN CAT: EXACTLY NOW FIX IT

      9. Just_Dropping_By says:
        December 3, 2015 at 12:01 pm

        The fussiness of cats about the quality of water in their water dishes is one of the things that I find most bizarre from an evolutionary standpoint. House cats are believed to be domesticated from the African wildcat, which are native to arid and semiarid locations. Logically, one would think that would make house cats less picky about water quality because, in the wild, water sources would be rather infrequent and often not of the best quality, but that clearly doesn’t seem to be the case, based on anecdotal evidence at least. (This particularly stands out because the eating habits of domesticated cats and dogs are pretty much completely consistent with their ancestry — African wild cats are solitary hunters and can drag their prey off to consume it at their leisure, whereas wolves are pack hunters and if you don’t eat all that you possibly can right now, your pack mates are going to eat it, so you should “wolf it down” as fast as possible. Domesticated cats tend to be dainty eaters and it’s possible to put out a dish with multiple days’ worth of food and the cat will pace itself with it, whereas dogs usually consume every fleck of food in their bowl as fast as they can.)
