      Dear Conservatives, Stop extolling the moral superiority of Nazis, please?

      [ 121 ] November 19, 2015 | SEK

      I’m a Jew of German descent — if you don’t believe me, just ask my surname — whose family tree would have many more branches if it hadn’t been pruned by the Nazis. Many German Jews sought refuge in the United States as the Nazis came to power, but were denied for exactly the same reasons bandied about Syrians today — they’re likely spies, they’re not really white, they can’t be trusted, etc.

      That’s why Anne Frank lived in an attic and died in the camps. (Let’s not even mention the Japanese-Americans. Let’s leave that to George Takei.)

      Some have argued that it’s different now — that radical Muslims are unimaginably more horrible beasts than Nazis — but if you believe that, you’re a Nazi who believes that even the most mass-murderous of mass-murdering fucks to ever walk the Earth are somehow morally superior to the current crop of Islamic extremists.

      Because I’ve seen that argument made tonight — “We should have let Anne Frank in, because white German spies would’ve just behaved like spies, whereas these brown Muslims are terrorists through and through” — and I’ve witnessed it repeatedly, mouthed by people who don’t realize that they’re extolling the moral superiority of Nazis.

      That bears repeating: They’re extolling the moral superiority of Nazis.

      So here’s where we stand — if you believe that we shouldn’t allow Syrian refugees fleeing from ISIS into the country because you believe ISIS when it says it’s embedded terrorists in the refugee communities, you and I are done.

      Because even if ISIS isn’t lying — which it is, but whatever — you’re doing moral calculus in crayon on the walls outside your racist parents’ bedroom, and it’s unbecoming of a civilized adult society. If the price of humanitarianism is that a few rotten apples spoil a barrel, it doesn’t matter because American democracy is a fucking cargo ship and the other 100,000 barrels matter too.

        While I’m suspicious of my ability to productively participate, I think this objection to the Roanoke mayor’s statement from what seems to be their local paper deserves some discussion and analysis.

        It seems that what they’re opposing is not racist discrimination with violent/fatal consequences (they’re rather ambivalent on that, bordering on supportive), they simply oppose a moral equivalence between the actions they’re proposing/seeking to portray as legitimate and actions that are more uncontroversially incorrect. Likewise, their distaste for the mayor’s remark is not because they found it especially noxious on the merits, but because his comparison was vivid enough to directly outline the level of humane consideration Americans are willing to grant refugees. You’re a monster if you think internment camps are reasonable but you’re not a monster when you can phrase your willingness to let innocent refugees who want to avoid being tortured in prison cells, bombed by their government, hit with chemical weapons or drowned in their eagerness to escape the same in terms that share a sense of possible consensus and that separate your argument (we just want to avoid terrorists) from the actual effects of your action (we just want to leave Syrian people in Syria where all of that can happen).

        People seem shocked that Drum’s statement could be so strongly objected to — this is why. In presenting one of racism’s present faces in terms that paint it as a reasonable, understandable proposition that reasonable people could hold valid, differing opinions on, he legitimized — without much heed to the consequences — a strand of American political thought that will never, ever effect him. The paper, in its distaste for internment, reproduced (using Drum’s argument for cover) the sense that terrorism is omnipresent, saying that it “needs only a handful of people moving among us” which subtly implicates existing Muslims just as fully as it attempts to implicate the refugees. Parties that can call that position legitimate should own the action and own the consequences.

        Semi-relatedly, while I’m not fully willing to dismiss fear as a factor here, its dominance as a consideration is much overstated. A lot of this isn’t fear; it’s racist opportunism that’s using the possibility of fear as a pretext for acceptability. The same rhetorical environment that makes a paper strenuously trip over itself to condemn internment (while supporting death-by-torture just as long as it’s out of sight and out of mind) is the same rhetorical environment our politicians are operating in. The balancing act, as I see it, isn’t “racist vs not-racist” but “non-obviously racist vs obviously racist” (where “obviousness” is limited to things that are only uncontroversially bad to most white people now) and that will remain true as long as certain racist power plays implicitly request moral deniability. It should be further noted that parts of the electorate are no longer similarly constrained and that while it also values deniability, it values the substance of certain kinds of power being enacted more.
