      SEK on Graphic Policy Radio talking about Agent Carter

      [ 26 ] January 19, 2015 | SEK

      If you’re so inclined, you can listen to myself and Elana Levin and Brett Schenker discuss Marvel’s Agent Carter. There’s a special bonus for those of you who think I’m a hypocrite — I begin the podcast strongly espousing one opinion and end it opining quite to the contrary.

      Fun is had by all.

      Also, people who know better than I do didn’t think this was a totally ridiculous idea:

      #NickFury is a title, not a person. Our new @GraphicPolicy Radio theory. #marvelcu

                    • Abigail Nussbaum says:
                      January 20, 2015 at 3:45 pm

                      The question about the Avengers’ accountability is a good point, and there’s probably a longer discussion to be had about how the MCU presents them vs. how it presents SHIELD that leads to their lack of accountability being so much less problematic (which Murc gets into in his reply to you). But to me the core issue is one of the uncanny valley. Captain America and Iron Man are sufficiently alien to the world as it is that it doesn’t bother me so much that they’re essentially ungovernable. But SHIELD – and especially SHIELD as the MCU envisions it, which is basically a CIA that occasionally deals with aliens – is so similar to real-world organizations, and has such similar flaws, that I find it, and the characters’ glee at suborning any attempt by the government to control them, genuinely chilling. To put it another way, I don’t think the difference between NCIS‘s authority worship and AoS’s is as great as you suggest.

                      And yes, I absolutely see a thread of contempt for transparency and accountability running through the show, for example through the character of Skye, who starts the first season saying everything we’ve been saying about the people’s right to know and the scariness of SHIELD’s methods, and who – despite having spent the season getting a close look at SHIELD’s most unsavory traits – ends it sighing that the world “isn’t ready” to know what SHIELD was up to all those decades.

                      I think the scene that really drove home for me how messed up the show’s take on accountability is was the one where Maria Hill is seen complaining about the tediousness of testifying before congress about SHIELD and its projects. It’s a funny scene, and Cobie Smulders is fantastic as Hill so naturally you think she’s the best and congress are a bunch of party-poopers. But if you stop for a minute to listen to what she’s saying, you realize that this is a person who had tremendous unchecked power, abused it horribly (remember, even before the Hydra reveal, Hill was the buck’s next-to-last stop for approval on a project that would have given SHIELD the power to kill anyone on the planet with the push of a button), failed spectacularly at the only part of her job that actually justified the power she had, and is now royally pissed that she’s being called to account for any of this, and maligning the intelligence and capabilities of the elected officials who are doing so. Change her gender and add a few years and kilograms, and Hill could be any bank executive after the financial crisis, complaining that just because they tanked the economy doesn’t mean their bonuses should be taken away.

                      And the thing is, even if you accept that SHIELD is necessary, its existence is in no way incompatible with transparency and accountability. Especially in a world that is already aware of the existence of superpowers and aliens, there’s no reason for SHIELD not to function like any other law enforcement agency except that the MCU’s writers don’t think it would be as cool. The show keeps falling back on the notion that its characters are somehow exceptional, the only people capable of keeping the world safe, but really that’s only the case because they keep hoarding intelligence and technology in order to make themselves the only ones able to do the job. You can’t deliberately keep people in the dark for decades, and then use their ignorance as a justification for why you should be allowed to continue doing it.
