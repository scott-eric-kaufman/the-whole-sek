          Actually I kind of doubt anything more damaging to the Texas State Police has been cut (or at least intentionally) and anything that tends to help the officer (there is not much) has been included.

          This is the Texas State Police – they have good legal counsel and good PR advisors. The advice I’d give them is simple – the entire video is a matter of record, it is going to come out sooner or later. If you try to edit it to take out transgressions by the officer, you will be in trouble – so get it all out at once.

          Although there is no evidence [yet] of murder, there is a lot in this video.

          Having looked at it, in an evenhanded world it would support in sequence:

          a. A charge against the officer of dangerous driving – the speed with which he approached her was excessive and forced her to change lanes. Remember he charged her for the lane change, so he had no justification for the prior driving behaviour.

          b. Assault – the threat to “light her up” and the improper use of the taser (he was wrong to demand that she exit the vehicle at that late point in a protracted stop and no right to insist she put out the cigarette) – threatening someone in this way is assault (Texas law: “the defendant intentionally or knowingly threatened the victim with imminent bodily injury.”)

          c. Battery – he had no grounds to remove her from the vehicle – so his grabbing her and violently flinging her to the ground was battery. (Again Texas law: “The defendant intentionally or knowingly caused physical contact with the victim when the defendant knew or should have reasonably believed that the victim would regard the contact as offensive.” and “The defendant intentionally, knowingly or recklessly caused bodily injury to the victim”)

          d. Given the brandishing of a weapon, the injuries she may have sustained and the apparent abuse of authority, this may rise to the level of aggravated assault.

          e. It does appear that the officer’s police report included false information, or at least did not include relevant details. That is potentially an offence under ยง37.08 of the Texas penal code.

          f. If the false report was used to detain her, then he faces potential charges of false arrest, false imprisonment to kidnapping.

          Here is what seems to have happened – the officer realised after he had her in cuffs that he had a serious problem – a lose your job, get sued and lose sort of problem. Then, with or without collusion by more senior officers, a decision is taken to put her in jail for the weekend and hopefully more, so that her “attitude” could be adjusted to the point that she would “admit” assault on the officer. Interestingly she is thrown in a small town Texas jail, at the weekend – with her family in Illinois, something that even on a weekday would complicate organising bail or bonding. It would be interesting to review other dash cam videos of this particular officer – 1 year on the force, I suspect a pattern would emerge.

              • dilan says:

                One of the big problems with all of this is that police have way too much power at the “lawful order” stage.

                A police officer is presumptively equal to a citizen. Having the power to compel someone to do something under threat of violence is a grave power that police should not be able to wield willy-nilly. Absent a chain of facts in which the police officer acted properly at all stages and where an arrestable offense has occurred, a lawful search may be conducted, or an imminent threat to safety is present, every order by a police officer should be considered unlawful.

                In this case, the fact that the trigger for the order was her refusal to put out her cigarette, his order to get out of the car should, under a fair legal system, be considered unlawful, and she should have every right to say “no”, and his response to her saying “no” should be to back down. I realize that is not the law now, but that is what it should be.

                Ordering citizens around is what bad cops get off on. It should be something they should be permitted to do only rarely and in strictly defined circumstances.
