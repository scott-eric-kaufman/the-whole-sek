        • NewishLawyer says:
          March 13, 2014 at 10:39 am

          To further expand on Rea’s comments. In the old common law system, felonies were narrowly defined and there were only about 5-7 of them. The reason for this narrow definition is that many to all convicted felons were automatically given the death sentence and often fairly quickly as far as I can tell.

          As society progressed, we began getting queasier and queasier about a broad death penalty (at least most of us). Michigan was one of the first state’s to ban the death penalty across the board in the 1800s. Pennsylvania introduced degrees of murder to help limit the application of the death penalty in the late 1700s or early 1800s.

          We have degrees of crime (for all crimes) to help establish proper sentencing and make sure that things are not overly broad and harsh.

      10. Crunchy Frog says:
        March 13, 2014 at 10:30 am

        About 12 years ago our then-8 year old was assaulted (not sexually) repeatedly by her special ed teacher who also had been a “Teacher of the Year”. The district covered it up, eventually under pressure sent her to another district with no warning. That other district figured her out in about 2 months and fired her for cause, effectively ending her career.

        But because our district wouldn’t address the cover-up problems (we eventually learned that this teacher had had a very long history of such complaints) we sued and, after 3 years, won. During the trial the defense really tried to portray the teacher as a “Teacher of the Year” with all kinds of pro-character witnesses, and safe in the knowledge that they’d destroyed all records and that we couldn’t introduce evidence from her subsequent behavior.

        Alas, the defense overplayed their hand. First, they successfully put a lot of teachers on the jury, thinking that the teachers would identify with the defendant – but when the teachers heard that no documentation existed they knew a cover up had occurred. Second, they put the teacher on the stand last, with a tightly rehearsed testimony, but because they didn’t believe the charges they didn’t account for her propensity to embellish her lies spontaneously. Furthermore, they timed her testimony to be last in the week, forcing the cross-ex to the next week, with the idea that the jury would spend the weekend in the glow of her greatness. But that gave us time to research her testimony in detail, giving us some Perry Mason moments in the cross-ex. (One justification for throwing our child to the floor that she invented on the spot was a heat wave that meant children couldn’t go outside – turned out it was unusually cool that day. In the cross-ex we let her embellish the heat wave lie with all kinds of details before submitting the weather report that day as evidence.) As she walked off the stand following cross-ex the jury were sending her looks that could kill.

        Of course, winning the case didn’t mean all was better. The leaders in the administration all got big retirement and/or severance packages, the lawyers cleaned up the large majority of the settlement, and despite some helpful efforts by George Miller and others in Congress no laws have been changed to avoid repeats of this situation.
