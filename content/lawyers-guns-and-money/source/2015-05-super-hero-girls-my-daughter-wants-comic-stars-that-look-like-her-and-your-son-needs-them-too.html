      Super Hero Girls: My daughter wants comic stars that look like her — and your son needs them, too

      [ 32 ] May 1, 2015 | SEK

      A fascinating article by my friend David Perry, and one I know is of interest to Farley (since I saw him post about this issue on Facebook last week) and which I thought might be of interest to y’all as well. Sample:

      Comics matter. They have become the dominant genre for depicting heroism in mass media. This dominance spills from Hollywood to television, toys, apparel, and more. When children imagine the heroic, they are influenced by the major brands like Marvel (owned by Disney) and DC. This puts a lot of pressure on these creators to get things right, and when it comes to gender, they mostly are doing a terrible job.

      Every time a major movie involving super heroes comes out, fans ask – where are the female characters? Guardians of the Galaxymerchandising sparked a “where’s Gamora?” campaign. A producer of Big Hero 6 merchandise left the female characters off because, “Eeeww girls! Yuck! Haha.” Fans of the new Avengers: Age of Ultron movie are complaining that Black Widow rarely appears in the official licensed products. Irritated fans have coalesced online under the hashtags #WheresNatasha and #IncludeTheGirls. Irritated fans have coalesced online under the hashtags #WheresNatasha and #IncludeTheGirls. Even Mark Ruffalo (who plays Hulk) Tweeted, “@Marvel we need more #BlackWidow merchandise for my daughters and nieces. Pretty please.”

      Sometimes the sexism is overt. Both DC and Marvel have licensed products that suggest girls should be love interests, not heroes themselves. DC has shirts saying “I only date superheroes” and “training to the Batman’s girlfriend.” Marvel released a shirt showing four Avengers bursting out of the chest and likewise reading “I only date superheroes.” Marvel also released a product line in which a boys’ shirt said, “Be a Hero” and the girls’ reads, “I need a hero.” Let’s be clear, when my daughter goes outside to fight bad guys, she doesn’t need a hero. She is one.

      The pattern is obvious – female characters from Disney (which owns Marvel) and DC are under-marketed. The few products exist in segregated “girls only” categories and often reflect sexist ideologies. What was unusual about the Big Hero 6 “Eeeww girls” comment was that the spokesperson said aloud what clearly most marketing executives are all thinking – add a single girl to a product, and boys just won’t buy it. Moreover, while the companies apologize for sexist products, they never seem to investigate the corporate structures that allowed such products to be created in the first place…

      3. Murc says:
        May 1, 2015 at 3:31 pm

        There are two major problems here at a “practical” level. You have two big players in this game, DC and Marvel. What that really means is you have Warner Brothers and Disney.

        Warner Brothers is simply straight-up hostile to women and enters into partnerships with media outlets that are also hostile to women. Young Justice and Green Lantern (the cartoons) were both cancelled by Cartoon Network because they were explicitly supposed to be vehicles for selling toys to boys, and their viewership ended up skewing way more female than the execs liked, and said execs are locked into a mindset that girls buy dolls, boys buy action figures.

        (Paul Dini had a lot to say on this topic.)

        So that’s Warner Brothers. Now we have Disney.

        Disney is much less hostile to women as a market in general than Warner Brothers is (low bar to clear) but they still have their marketing divided into distinct streams, and, more importantly, their “Princess” stuff sucks a lot of oxygen out of the room. For that, it’s a rather naked cost-benefit calculation; they have no objection in principle to growing the market, but that would require effort they do not, currently, see as being worth it; they’d have to become conversant with a whole different way of doing things, and bring products to market they’re not all that familiar with and learn how they work. Why invest that kind of effort when they can market the princess lines to girls, the action hero lines to boys (both of which they understand) cash the checks, and move on?

        It’s a real problem.

        • burritoboy says:
          May 3, 2015 at 11:05 am

          To me, it’s actually more interesting that other liberals don’t understand this better yet. The institutional investors are just a market like any other. Companies will tell them what the companies believe the investors can already easily understand. That is, they want their company to look like what the investors a priori imagine a “good” / profitable / growing company to look like. This is not only true for struggling companies, but many companies who are doing well often find they need to communicate stories to investors that are only parts of the truth, as many investors simply can’t understand more of the total picture of the company.

          And what investors imagine a “good” company to look like is no more rationally calculated than anything else. It’s a mix of hot business trends, easily absorbed ideas, prejudices, half-remembered nostrums from school, etc. What Disney investors want to see is (most likely) that Disney makes a new product as similar as possible to things they have already seen Disney do well and to have that new product produce a surprisingly large stream of revenue. Thus, the new Disney superhero movie should look a lot like the last one, but (for whatever reason) makes more revenue than expected.
